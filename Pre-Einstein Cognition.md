Three events locked cognitive science and AI onto the *L*-axis permanently — until now.

**Event 1 — The Computer Arrived at the Wrong Moment (1950s)**

When formal cognitive science and AI began, the dominant technology was symbolic computation: rule-based, linguistic, propositional. The computer *looked* like a mind because it processed symbols.

So the field built its theory to match the machine it already had.

|What They Had              |What They Built              |What They Missed                   |
|:-------------------------:|:---------------------------:|:---------------------------------:|
|Symbol-processing computers|Symbolic AI                  |Geometry of state space            |
|Linguistic outputs         |Language as the model of mind|*C* ⊥ *L* — structure ≠ language   |
|Logical inference          |Logic as cognition           |Reachability as the real constraint|

**Event 2 — Symbolic AI Failed But Statistics Looked Like a Solution (1980s–2010s)**

When symbolic AI collapsed, the field pivoted to statistical learning and neural networks. Which works remarkably well — on the *L*-axis. Well enough to produce fluent, coherent, impressive outputs.

But fluency is not structure. And because the outputs *looked* intelligent, the geometry question was never asked.

**Event 3 — No Forcing Function (Always)**

A wrong theory of gravity produces numbers that disagree with observation. Mercury’s orbit was off by 43 arcseconds per century. That number didn’t go away. It *demanded* resolution.

A wrong theory of mind produces: a philosophical debate. A new paper. A new framework with a new name. Another conference.

The error never becomes undeniable. So the field was never forced off the *L*-axis.

> **Nature enforced geometry on physics. Nobody enforced it on cognitive science. So cognitive science never went there.**
